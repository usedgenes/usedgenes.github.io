Frequently Asked Questions

Why do you want to study electrical engineering?

I find electrical engineering to be so incredibly fascinating because it sits at the foundation of so many modern technologies, with just how widespread electronics are. Through EE, I can better understand how everything that makes our world run works, from radios to computers to our power grids. What excites me in particular though is the precision and skill involved in chip manufacturing. The fact that we are able to work at nanometer scales with so little margin for error is just jaw-dropping, like it's the culmination of all the technological advancements ever made. It feels like there's a constant drive for innovation, that even if Moore's Law might not hold anymore, we can still find alternatives to progress even further.
Plus, I love EE words like MOSFETs, diodes, transistors, etc.; they're just so fun to say!

What is your biggest strength?

I'd have to say that my biggest strength is my adaptability and determination. No matter what obstacles come in my way, I'm always able to calmly evaluate the situation and figure out a way to overcome the challenge. This skill is particularly valuable in electrical engineering, since debugging circuits and code is such an integral part of this field. For instance, in one of the first PCBs I built, I mistakenly routed a trace from a sensor output pin to an MCU pin that was output only, preventing the MCU from reading the data. So, I carefully soldered a wire between the tiny MCU pins and the sensor, then secured everything with a generous amount of hot glue. While this solution was quite sketchy, the MCU did end up being able to read the sensor data, helping me save the entire PCB.

What advice would you give to incoming EE students?

Find what makes you excited about EE! Whether that be circuit tech like me, power systems, robotics, or anything in between. At least personally, I find that being genuinely interested in a subject you will make learning so much faster and more memorable. Besides that, try to do projects with things like Arduinos and PCBs to independently learn some EE concepts. Not only will this deepen your understanding and make you enjoy EE even more, but it'll also teach you how to learn, a skill that's honestly more important than any technical skill a class can teach you. Also, definitely take some CS classes on the side, as EE and CS are basically intertwined. 

What is your preferred programming language?

Definitely Java. It was the first (programming) language I ever learned, and I'm glad it was. Everything about it is intuitive and logical, and it has a great balance between speed vs complexity in my opinion. Overall, it's just an extremely well-rounded language. You don't have to keep on fiddling with library versions like you always do with Python, you don't have to worry about memory management like with C/C++, and things actually make sense unlike with JavaScript. And plus, Java runs on over 3 billion devices! How cool is that?!
